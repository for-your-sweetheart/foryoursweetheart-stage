Diabetes and Ramadan Fasting

Dr Sueziani Binte Zainudin

MBBS (Singapore), MRCP (UK), MRCPS (Glasgow), FAMS

Consultant Endocrinology, Sengkang General Hospital

Email: sueziani.zainudin@singhealth.com.sg

Introduction

During the holy month of Ramadan, billions of Muslim adherents observe this ordained fast as one of the 5 central pillars of Islam. Although it provides spiritual uplift, this observance poses physical, psychological and social challenges which would be magnified in those with Diabetes Mellitus. Although Islam has provision for exemption from fasting on medical grounds, many people prefer to fast. It is estimated that more than 116 million people with diabetes fast during Ramadan2. This is ultimately each personâ€™s decision. Hence, people with diabetes need to undergo pre-Ramadan counselling with proper education and adjustment of treatment to encourage self-empowerment.

We are well aware that Islam provides exemption from fasting in the following circumstances:

  • the frail and elderly,
  • children up to the age of puberty,
  • those with a chronic condition whereby participating in fasting would be detrimental to their health,
  • those who are unable to understand the purpose of fasting such as learning difficulties or severe mental health problems,
  • travellers of a distance further than 50 miles
  • those acutely unwell,
  • menstruating women, and
  • pregnant and breastfeeding women.

When compensating for missed fasts is not possible due to chronic or critical illness, one must feed the poor via fidya.

Risks associated with Fasting in people with Diabetes

People with Diabetes who wish to fast in Ramadan are at increased risk of adverse events which are magnified with longer fasting periods. The risks include:

  • Hypoglycaemia (glucose < 3.9mmol/L)
  • Hyperglycaemia (glucose > 16.7mmol/L)
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis and
  • Dehydration and thrombosis.

In the event you experience any symptoms of hypoglycaemia or hyperglycemia or feel unwell, a glucose level check is necessary and you need to consider breaking fast before consulting a nearby clinic for further treatment.  

Risk Stratification of people with Diabetes during fasting

The decision for a person with diabetes to fast is ultimately an important personal decision which should be made having considered the guidelines for religious exemptions and associated individualâ€™s risks in consultation with your healthcare provider. Although frequently recommended not to fast, if you still wish to fast, you should discuss this with your healthcare provider and develop a plan.

Pre-Ramadan assessment and Ramadan-focused Diabetes education

All people with Diabetes planning to fast should undergo a medical assessment and undergo a structured education program to undertake Ramadan fasting as safely as possible 6 to 8 weeks before Ramadan. It is crucial that your care is individualized. Assessment of your overall well-being in addition to control of glucose, blood pressure, cholesterol, and related diabetes complications should be assessed and further evaluate as needed as part of each individualâ€™s risk assessment. This assessment should include those not planning to fast as they are frequently exposed to similar risk in view of perceived social norms during the fasting month.

In addition, adjustment of diet and meal planning, adjustment of treatment regimen, encouragement of continued appropriate level of physical activity with appropriate timing, and recognition of warning symptoms and sick day advice, including monitoring your glucose level and not to fast if unwell but go to see a doctor, should be discussed.

Ramadan-focused structured education program decreases hypoglycaemia by almost 50% from baseline compared to 4-fold increase for those who did not undergo the education, with some weight loss compared to weight gain respectively10.

Management of lifestyle and glucose monitoring

During pre-Ramadan counselling, diet counselling and meal planning is necessary to promote stable glucose levels during Ramadan fasting. Inappropriate diet with over-eating, typically with large intake of sugary desserts, carbohydrate and fat at sunset meal should be avoided. Instead, nutritional advice with meal planning helps to aim for a healthy and balanced diet for maintaining body weight or promoting weight loss for those who are overweight. The pre-dawn meal should consist of complex, fibre-rich carbohydrates with low glycemic index diet to be taken as late as possible before the start of the daily fast, with increased intake of non-caffeinated unsweetened fluids during non-fasting hours.

In addition, you should maintain normal levels of physical activity with avoidance of excessive activity especially in the few hours prior to the sunset meal.

Blood testing do not invalidate fasting and should be performed several times daily especially in afternoon and in those requiring insulin. You should immediately end your fast and contact your healthcare provider if your blood glucose is less than 3.9mmol/L, and also if your blood glucose is more than 16.7mmol/L.

Medication adjustment for Ramadan fasting

During pre-Ramadan counselling, changes in treatment regimen should be made with test fasting to ensure stable regimen prior to initiation of fast during Ramadan.

Even those well-controlled on lifestyle are at risk of high blood sugars after meals. Hence, the caloric intake needs to be redistributed over 2 to 3 smaller meals during non-fasting period to avoid excessive rise in glucose levels.

If you are on medication with higher risk of causing hypoglycaemia such as glibenclamide, it needs to be changed to a medication that is less likely to cause hypoglycaemia such as gliclazide or other medication such as repaglinide, metformin, pioglitazone, acarbose, linagliptin, empagliflozin or liraglutide which are less likely to cause hypoglycaemia, which could be taken before sunset and pre-dawn meals.

If you are on insulin treatment, you are at high risk of hypoglycaemia, hence, you need to modify your regimen to reduce their risk. This dose you should be using depends on your pre-existing glucose control and glucose levels as you monitor them as well as your meal times and sizes. Hence, this is best adjusted together with your healthcare team. Insulin injection also does not invalidate fasting and can be given during the fasting hours.

In prolonged fasting with excessive water loss, dehydration may occur with increased risk of low blood pressure. Hence, dosage of medication for high blood pressure and diuretics may need to be adjusted or withheld.

Ramadan Fasting during pregnancy

Although Ramadan fasting is exempted during pregnancy, a local study reported that 87% continued to fast14. If you are a pregnant diabetes patient who plans to fast, you will need pre-Ramadan evaluation and close monitoring in high-risk clinics with appropriate dietary counselling and intensive insulin therapy. Frequent blood glucose monitoring coupled with feedback for frequent insulin dose adjustment to maintain near-normal blood glucose is necessary to avert complications to the mother and baby.

Fasting during COVID-19 pandemic

There is not enough evidence to show that people with diabetes are more likely to get COVID-19 than the general population. However, people with diabetes face a higher risk of experiencing serious complications from COVID-19 infection. The risk is even higher in those with diabetes-related complications such as heart disease, other vascular conditions or kidney problems.  Generally, in most people with diabetes fasting doesnâ€™t constitute extra risks to them to get infection.

People with Diabetes should follow the general advice for COVID-19. You should stay at home and avoid crowded places. You should be vaccinated according to the recommendations, adjust medication as advised and adhere to treatment prescribed.

Conclusion

Whilst Ramadan fasting can prove to be a challenge for people with diabetes, with appropriate preparation and assessment by your healthcare provider before Ramadan, and regular monitoring during Ramadan fasting, you can fast more safely. Do consult your healthcare provider today to enjoy a much safer and fulfilling Ramadan this year.

And remember to continue your good habits during the Raya festive season after Ramadan too!

Summary take-home points

 

  • Pre-Ramadan counselling: risk stratification, optimisation of glucose control, adjustment of treatment and diet regimen, monitoring of physical activity, glucose monitoring with trial of fasting, recognition of hypoglycemia and hyperglycemias as well as sick day advice
  • During Ramadan: institute stable treatment and diet regimen, with frequent monitoring of blood glucose, warning signs, and monitoring of physical activity level
  • Post-Ramadan: review treatment regimen and experience during Ramadan fasting
